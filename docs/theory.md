## 1. Why This Is a Multivariate Time-Series Problem

### 1.1 The Data Structure

The cultivation intelligence problem does not reduce cleanly to a standard supervised learning setup. The data generated by the Legacy Ag facility is fundamentally **multivariate time-series data**: a set of N concurrent sensor streams, each producing a reading at regular intervals, where the streams are correlated with each other and where the values at time t are statistically dependent on values at times t-1, t-2, ..., t-k.

The sensor streams monitored by the platform include:

| Stream | Symbol | Typical Cadence |
|---|---|---|
| Air temperature | T_air | 60 s |
| Relative humidity | RH | 60 s |
| Vapour pressure deficit | VPD | 60 s (derived) |
| CO₂ concentration | [CO₂] | 60 s |
| PPFD (photosynthetic photon flux density) | PPFD | 60 s |
| Daily light integral | DLI | Daily integral |
| Substrate volumetric water content | VWC | 5 min |
| Nutrient solution electrical conductivity | EC | 5 min |
| Nutrient solution pH | pH | 5 min |
| Rootzone temperature | T_root | 5 min |
| Dosing volume (AquaPro AQU1AD04A42) | V_dose | Event-triggered |

These streams are not independent. VPD is a deterministic function of T_air and RH (Section 6.1). EC in the rootzone is influenced by T_root, irrigation timing (V_dose), and plant uptake rate — all of which vary across grow stage. pH and EC interact because many nutrients exhibit pH-dependent solubility. PPFD drives stomatal aperture, which affects transpiration rate, which affects VWC drawdown rate between irrigations.

### 1.2 Non-Stationarity Across Grow Stages

A critical and frequently underappreciated complication is that the statistical properties of all sensor streams **change systematically across grow stages**. This is non-stationarity in the structural sense: the data-generating process itself changes.

Consider VPD. A value of 1.2 kPa during the propagation stage might indicate optimal conditions for young cuttings. The same VPD during late flower might be slightly sub-optimal — late-flowering plants typically benefit from slightly higher VPD (1.2–1.5 kPa) to manage trichome integrity and reduce late-stage botrytis pressure. A naive model trained without grow-stage awareness would treat these identically.

The four primary grow stages — propagation, vegetative, early flower, and late flower — differ not only in environmental targets but in:

- Plant canopy density and therefore transpiration rate per unit floor area
- Nutrient demand (N-heavy in veg, P/K-heavy in flower)
- Sensitivity to environmental excursions (young plants in propagation are disproportionately sensitive)
- DLI targets (lower in propagation, ramping through veg, peak in early flower, sometimes reduced in late flower)
- Acceptable EC ranges (lower in propagation, higher in late veg and flower)

Any model that does not explicitly account for grow stage will learn spurious cross-stage correlations.

### 1.3 Sparse Event Labels and Temporal Dependencies

Grow batch outcomes — yield index, cannabinoid profile variance, batch pass/fail rate — are observed **once per batch**. A batch runs for 60–90 days. The label is a single observation at the end of that period, summarising the integrated effect of thousands of environmental readings. This creates an extreme case of label sparsity relative to the volume of feature data.

Furthermore, environmental actions have lagged effects. A nutrient adjustment made on day 35 of flower affects rootzone EC over the following 48–72 hours. An EC excursion on day 20 of flower may not manifest as visible plant stress until day 25–28. A model that treats each time step as independent cannot capture these dynamics.

**Practical consequence:** Models must be designed around features that explicitly capture temporal context — rolling statistics, lag features, cumulative integrals — rather than instantaneous readings alone.

### 1.4 Sparse Batch Labels

At the time of Phase 1 model training, the facility has approximately 10–20 completed batches with reliable environmental records and quality-assured outcome labels. This is a severely data-limited regime for any learning algorithm.

To put this in context: a batch record spanning 90 days at 1-minute resolution produces approximately 130,000 raw sensor readings per stream. After feature engineering, a single batch might be represented by ~500–1,000 engineered features (rolling statistics, lag values, stage summaries). With 15 batch-level labels, the effective supervised learning problem has 15 observations and up to 1,000 candidate features — a ratio that is guaranteed to produce overfitting in any sufficiently flexible model.

This data regime defines the entire modelling strategy described in Section 2.

## 2. Why Tabular Models Outperform Deep Models at Low Batch Counts

### 2.1 The Empirical Evidence

The seminal benchmarking study by Grinsztajn, Oyallon, and Varoquaux (2022), "Why tree-based models still outperform deep learning on tabular data," evaluated a large collection of real-world tabular datasets across multiple domains and found that gradient-boosted tree models (specifically XGBoost and LightGBM) consistently outperformed deep learning approaches including MLPs, ResNets, and TabNet, particularly on datasets with fewer than 10,000 samples and irregular feature distributions.

The authors identified several structural reasons:

1. **Irregular target functions.** Decision trees can represent arbitrary non-monotonic relationships between features and targets. Neural networks learn smooth functions by default and require significant data to fit irregular boundaries.
2. **Uninformative features.** Real-world tabular datasets frequently contain features with low or conditional relevance. Tree models naturally perform feature selection through splitting criteria. Neural networks require explicit regularisation to ignore irrelevant features.
3. **Mixed feature types.** Tabular data often mixes continuous, categorical, and binary features. Trees handle this natively; neural networks require careful preprocessing.

At the batch counts available for this facility (10–20 labelled examples at the batch level), the performance gap between tree models and deep learning is expected to be substantial. A well-engineered LightGBM model with appropriate regularisation will generalise better than any neural architecture in this regime.

### 2.2 The Bias-Variance Tradeoff

The bias-variance decomposition of generalisation error is:

```
E[(y - f̂(x))²] = Bias[f̂(x)]² + Var[f̂(x)] + σ²
```

where σ² is irreducible noise. In low-data regimes, variance dominates. Deep neural networks are high-variance models — they have enormous parameter counts relative to training samples, and without massive amounts of data, they overfit severely. Dropout, L2 regularisation, and early stopping mitigate but do not eliminate this problem.

Gradient boosted trees with appropriate regularisation (max_depth, min_child_samples, subsample, colsample_bytree) and a low learning rate have substantially lower variance at low sample counts. The ensemble nature of boosted trees provides intrinsic regularisation through aggregation across weak learners.

**Practical consequence:** For batch-level outcome prediction with N ≈ 10–20, a LightGBM model with 100–500 trees, max_depth of 3–5, and aggressive regularisation will generalise better than any neural approach, regardless of architecture sophistication.

### 2.3 Feature Engineering Encodes Domain Priors

One of the most powerful arguments for a tabular model approach in this domain is that **feature engineering allows domain expertise to be incorporated directly into the learning problem**, reducing the learning burden on the model.

Rather than asking a neural network to discover from scratch that the average VPD during weeks 3 and 4 of flower is more predictive of late-flower botrytis risk than the average VPD during the entire batch, we encode this prior directly as a feature:

```python
features["vpd_mean_flower_w3w4"] = batch_data.query(
    "grow_stage == 'early_flower' and week_of_stage in [3, 4]"
)["vpd"].mean()
```

Other examples of domain-prior features:

- `dli_deficit_veg_cumulative`: total shortfall from DLI target during vegetative stage (integral of max(0, DLI_target - DLI_actual) over veg days)
- `ec_excursion_count_flower`: number of 6-hour windows in flower stage where EC was outside the target range
- `vpd_stress_hours_propagation`: hours in propagation where VPD exceeded 1.0 kPa
- `ph_variance_week1_flower`: variance of pH readings in the first 7 days of flower (high variance indicates dosing instability)
- `irrigation_frequency_late_flower`: mean irrigations per day in late flower (proxy for substrate moisture management quality)

Each of these features embeds years of agronomic knowledge about what matters when. A neural network would need hundreds of batches to discover these patterns from raw sensor data. The tree model needs them as inputs.

### 2.4 Interpretability as a Feature

SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) values provide mathematically grounded feature importance attributions for individual predictions. For a batch outcome prediction:

```
prediction = base_value + Σᵢ SHAP_value(feature_i)
```

where the SHAP values sum to the difference between the prediction and the base value (mean prediction across training data). Each SHAP value represents the marginal contribution of that specific feature to the specific prediction.

This is operationally critical for adoption. A recommendation that says "Yield index predicted at 78% of target — primary contributing factors: (1) VPD stress hours in propagation +4.2%, (2) EC excursion count in early flower +3.8%, (3) DLI deficit in late veg +2.1%" is actionable. A neural network's internal representations are not interpretable in this way without additional post-hoc methods that are themselves approximate.

The Head Grower (Persona 1) will evaluate every recommendation against their own experience. A system that cannot explain its reasoning will not be trusted. Explainability is not a nice-to-have — it is a prerequisite for adoption.

### 2.5 Probability Calibration

Well-calibrated probability estimates are essential for the risk scoring system. A risk score of 80/100 should mean that approximately 80% of batches with this risk score experienced an adverse outcome. This requires that the model's predicted probabilities are calibrated — that they correspond to empirical frequencies.

Tree models are better calibrated than neural networks without explicit calibration steps (Niculescu-Mizil and Caruana, 2005). In LightGBM, calibration can be further improved with `CalibratedClassifierCV` (isotonic regression) in scikit-learn. For the small sample sizes in this problem, calibration on the training set is risky; cross-validated calibration should be used.

## 3. When TCN / LSTM / Transformer IS Justified

The argument in Section 2 is not that deep learning is never appropriate for this domain — it is that deep learning is not appropriate *now*, given current batch counts. There are specific problem formulations where neural sequence models become justified and eventually superior.

### 3.1 Multi-Step Ahead Sensor Forecasting

The batch outcome prediction problem (Section 2) operates at the batch level: N ≈ 15 observations. But there is a separate problem at the time-step level: **forecasting future sensor trajectories**.

"Given the current temperature, humidity, and CO₂ readings, what will VPD, EC, and VWC be in 1 hour? 4 hours? 12 hours?" This is a multi-step ahead multivariate forecasting problem. The number of training examples here is not 15 (batches) but approximately 50,000–100,000 (time steps across all batches), since each time step is an independent forecasting problem.

At this data volume, neural sequence models become competitive and can eventually outperform feature-engineered tree models by learning cross-sensor dynamics implicitly without hand-crafted features.

### 3.2 Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT)

The Temporal Fusion Transformer (Lim et al., 2021, "Temporal Fusion Transformers for Interpretable Multi-horizon Time Series Forecasting") is the recommended architecture for multi-horizon forecasting in this domain once sufficient data is available. Key properties that make it well-suited:

1. **Multi-horizon outputs.** TFT produces quantile forecasts at multiple horizons simultaneously (e.g., 1h, 4h, 12h ahead), rather than requiring a separate model per horizon.
2. **Known future inputs.** The model explicitly handles inputs that are known in the future (e.g., lighting schedule, planned photoperiod changes) separately from inputs that are observed only at the current time step. This is critical for cultivation forecasting — the lighting schedule for the next 24 hours is always known.
3. **Static covariates.** TFT incorporates static metadata (strain, room ID, batch ID) as conditioning inputs without treating them as time series.
4. **Attention-based interpretability.** The temporal self-attention mechanism produces attention weights that indicate which historical time steps the model relied on for each horizon prediction — a form of temporal interpretability.
5. **Quantile regression.** TFT is trained with quantile loss, producing prediction intervals directly rather than requiring a separate calibration step.

**Recommendation:** Evaluate TFT for environmental forecasting once Phase 1 batch data reaches the 6-month mark (approximately 75,000+ time steps at 1-minute resolution per sensor).

### 3.3 Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN)

Bai, Kolter, and Koltun (2018), "An Empirical Evaluation of Generic Convolutional and Recurrent Networks for Sequence Modeling," demonstrated that dilated causal convolutions (TCN architecture) match or exceed LSTM performance on a wide range of sequence modelling tasks, with significant advantages in parallelisability (all time steps computed simultaneously during training, unlike sequential LSTM) and stable gradients (no vanishing gradient problem).

For within-batch anomaly detection — detecting that a sensor stream is deviating from its expected trajectory in a way that cannot be explained by known stage transitions — a TCN trained on the multi-dimensional sensor state vector at 1-minute resolution is a viable future approach. The dilated convolution structure can efficiently capture long-range temporal dependencies (up to 2^k time steps with k dilated layers) without the computational overhead of attention mechanisms.

**Recommendation:** Evaluate TCN for real-time anomaly detection as an alternative to statistical process control methods once time-step-level data exceeds 100,000 observations.

### 3.4 LSTM for Batch Trajectory Modelling

At batch counts of approximately 50–100, an LSTM encoder that reads the environmental trajectory up to a given point in the batch and produces a latent representation of "batch state so far" becomes viable for batch outcome prediction. This is preferable to the feature engineering approach because:

1. The LSTM can learn which temporal patterns are predictive without explicit feature construction.
2. The model can update its outcome prediction continuously as new data arrives, rather than requiring fixed-length feature windows.
3. Transfer learning from models trained on similar facilities becomes possible.

**Trigger condition:** Evaluate LSTM batch trajectory models when the facility has 50+ completed batches with quality-assured outcome labels. Estimated timeframe: 18–24 months from Phase 1 deployment.

## 4. Uncertainty Quantification

### 4.1 Why Point Predictions Are Dangerous

A model that predicts "Yield index: 81%" without any indication of its confidence provides misleading precision. In a batch that the model has seen data similar to (within the training distribution), 81% might be accurate to ±3%. In a batch with unusual conditions (a new strain, a room configuration change, a dosing anomaly), the same 81% might have a true error of ±25%.

If the operator treats both predictions identically — because they are presented identically — the second case is an accident waiting to happen. Point predictions suppress the information that would allow the operator to weight the recommendation appropriately.

In a control context, if model predictions are used to trigger interventions, uncalibrated confidence leads to overreaction during high-uncertainty conditions and underreaction when the model is certain. Both error modes are costly.

### 4.2 Conformal Prediction

Conformal prediction (Vovk, Gammerman, and Shafer, 2005) provides **distribution-free coverage guarantees**: for a user-specified coverage level α (e.g., 90%), the conformal prediction interval is guaranteed to contain the true outcome with probability ≥ α, regardless of the true data distribution.

The conformal prediction algorithm for regression:

1. **Calibration set.** Reserve a calibration set of n examples from the training data (not used for fitting the model).
2. **Compute non-conformity scores.** For each calibration example (xᵢ, yᵢ), compute the residual: `sᵢ = |yᵢ - f̂(xᵢ)|`
3. **Compute the quantile.** Let q̂ be the ⌈(n+1)(1-α)/n⌉-th smallest non-conformity score.
4. **Prediction interval.** For a new test point x, the prediction interval is `[f̂(x) - q̂, f̂(x) + q̂]`.

This interval has the guarantee that P(y ∈ [f̂(x) - q̂, f̂(x) + q̂]) ≥ 1 - α under the assumption of exchangeability (i.e., the calibration and test data are drawn from the same distribution).

**Limitation:** At batch counts of N ≈ 15, the calibration set is very small, making the conformal intervals wide. This is appropriate — it accurately reflects genuine uncertainty — but operationally the intervals may be too wide to be actionable. As batch count grows, calibration set size grows and interval width decreases.

**Implementation:** The MAPIE library (Model Agnostic Prediction Interval Estimator) provides scikit-learn compatible conformal prediction wrappers for any regressor.

### 4.3 Quantile Regression in LightGBM

LightGBM supports quantile regression natively via `objective="quantile"` and `alpha=q`. Training separate models for the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentiles produces a distribution-free prediction interval without the calibration set requirement of conformal prediction.

The quantile loss function (also known as the pinball loss) for quantile q is:

```
L_q(y, ŷ) = q * max(y - ŷ, 0) + (1-q) * max(ŷ - y, 0)
```

The 10th and 90th percentile models jointly define an 80% prediction interval. Unlike conformal prediction, quantile regression does not provide a formal coverage guarantee — the intervals are calibrated by the training data distribution and may be poorly calibrated out-of-distribution.

**Recommendation:** Use quantile regression as the primary UQ method during Phase 1 (low batch count) due to its simplicity and integration with LightGBM. Supplement with conformal calibration as a secondary check when the calibration set grows to at least 5 examples.

### 4.4 Ensemble Disagreement

An ensemble of M LightGBM models trained on different bootstrap samples (or with different random seeds) produces M predictions for each test point. The variance across ensemble members is a natural measure of prediction uncertainty:

```
σ²_ensemble(x) = (1/M) * Σₘ (f̂ₘ(x) - μ̂(x))²
```

where μ̂(x) = (1/M) * Σₘ f̂ₘ(x) is the ensemble mean prediction. High variance indicates that the model is sensitive to training data composition at this test point — a reliable signal of high uncertainty.

This approach is sometimes called a "deep ensemble" (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) when applied to neural networks, but is equally applicable to tree ensembles. It is computationally cheap for LightGBM since each model trains in seconds.

## 5. Bounded Control Theory and Feedback Loops

### 5.1 The Feedback Loop Problem

If model recommendations influence operator actions, and operator actions change the environment, and changed environments produce the future training data for the next model version, the system is a closed feedback loop. ML models trained naively in this setting suffer from **covariate shift under their own policy**: the training distribution (environments observed under the old policy) diverges from the deployment distribution (environments observed under the new, model-influenced policy).

This problem is well-understood in reinforcement learning as the "distribution shift" problem. In cultivation, it manifests as: "The model learned that high VPD in week 3 of flower predicts poor outcomes. Operators therefore always correct high VPD in week 3. The model never sees high-VPD week-3 data in future batches. Its estimate of the effect of high VPD in week 3 becomes unreliable because it is no longer supported by recent data."

### 5.2 PID vs. Model-Predictive Control

Classical proportional-integral-derivative (PID) control is the dominant paradigm in environmental control for cultivation. A PID controller computes a control output u(t) from the error signal e(t) = setpoint - measurement(t):

```
u(t) = Kp * e(t) + Ki * ∫₀ᵗ e(τ)dτ + Kd * de(t)/dt
```

PID is robust, simple, and well-understood. For single-variable control loops (temperature, CO₂), it performs well. Its limitations in cultivation:

1. **SISO assumption.** PID controls one variable at a time. The cultivation environment is MIMO (multiple-input, multiple-output) — VPD depends on both temperature and humidity, which are controlled by separate actuators.
2. **No lookahead.** PID reacts to current error; it does not anticipate predictable future disturbances (e.g., the temperature rise that will occur when the lights come on at 06:00).
3. **No stage awareness.** A PID controller has a fixed setpoint. Switching setpoints across grow stages requires explicit programming.

Model-predictive control (MPC) addresses these limitations by using a process model to optimise future control actions over a finite prediction horizon. For an N-step MPC:

```
min_{u₀,...,u_{N-1}} Σᵢ [(xᵢ - r)ᵀ Q (xᵢ - r) + uᵢᵀ R uᵢ]
subject to: xᵢ₊₁ = f(xᵢ, uᵢ), u_min ≤ uᵢ ≤ u_max, x_min ≤ xᵢ ≤ x_max
```

where x is the state vector (temperature, humidity, CO₂, etc.), r is the reference (setpoint), Q is the state cost matrix, R is the control cost matrix, and f is the process model.

Using the ML models developed in this platform as the process model f in an MPC framework is a natural long-term evolution. The current advisory-first phase builds the process model; MPC automation is a Phase 3+ consideration.

### 5.3 Advisory Mode as a Safe First Step

Advisory mode — where the ML system produces recommendations that a human must explicitly approve and act on — provides safety properties that fully automated control does not:

1. **Human in the loop catches distribution shift.** If the model recommends something that seems wrong to the Head Grower (because conditions are unusual), the human override prevents the bad action.
2. **No compounding errors.** A wrong recommendation that is rejected has zero effect on the environment. A wrong automated action changes the environment, which may trigger further automated actions in a cascade.
3. **Audit trail of model errors.** When operators override recommendations, their reasoning is logged. This provides labelled training data for identifying where the model is wrong, which is more valuable than unlabelled out-of-distribution data.
4. **Trust is built incrementally.** The team develops confidence in specific recommendation types before those recommendations are considered for automation.

### 5.4 The Exploration-Exploitation Problem

A model trained only on historical data is biased toward what has been done before. If the model recommends conditions similar to the historical average, and operators follow those recommendations, the model never learns whether different conditions might produce better outcomes. This is the exploration-exploitation tradeoff.

In the cultivation context, the appropriate approach is conservative: explicit experimental design of deliberate, small, monitored deviations from standard conditions in dedicated "experimental" batches, rather than automated exploration. This is safer (deviations are bounded and sanctioned), more interpretable (the experimental batch is labelled as such), and more compliant with quality assurance requirements.

### 5.5 Hard Constraints as Safety Layers

Any future automated control must be bounded by hard constraints that cannot be overridden by model recommendations under any circumstance:

| Parameter | Hard Lower Bound | Hard Upper Bound | Rationale |
|---|---|---|---|
| Air temperature | 18°C | 30°C | Below: growth arrest. Above: heat stress, fire risk. |
| VPD | 0.4 kPa | 2.0 kPa | Below: excess transpiration suppression. Above: severe wilting. |
| CO₂ | 400 ppm | 1800 ppm | Below: CO₂ depletion. Above: human safety concern. |
| Nutrient EC | 0.5 mS/cm | 3.5 mS/cm | Below: nutrient deficiency. Above: salt toxicity. |
| Nutrient pH | 5.2 | 7.0 | Outside range: complete nutrient lockout in some elements. |

These constraints are enforced at the actuator level, not by the ML model. The ML model may recommend within or near these bounds; the control layer enforces that no action violates them.

This is the principle of constrained optimisation in control systems: the ML model optimises for yield and quality within a feasible region defined by hard physiological and safety constraints.

Amodei et al. (2016), "Concrete Problems in AI Safety," formally define the **safe interruptibility** requirement: an automated system must be stoppable and overridable at all times by a human operator without disruption to the system's other functions. This is built into the CI architecture at the API level — every recommendation endpoint includes an `override` flag, and the ingestion pipeline operates independently of the recommendation layer.

## 6. Cultivation-Specific Modelling Challenges

### 6.1 VPD Calculation

Vapour Pressure Deficit is the difference between the saturation vapour pressure at a given temperature and the actual vapour pressure of the air. It is the primary driver of plant transpiration rate and, at high values, of stomatal closure and photosynthesis suppression.

**Saturation vapour pressure (Buck equation, 1981):**

```
eₛ(T) = 0.61121 * exp((18.678 - T/234.5) * T / (257.14 + T))  [kPa]
```

where T is temperature in °C.

**Actual vapour pressure:**

```
eₐ = RH/100 * eₛ(T)  [kPa]
```

**VPD:**

```
VPD = eₛ(T) - eₐ = eₛ(T) * (1 - RH/100)  [kPa]
```

**Note:** This formula uses leaf-level VPD approximation (assuming leaf temperature ≈ air temperature). More precise calculations account for leaf-to-air temperature differences, which in high-light environments can be 1–3°C due to radiative heating. For indoor LED cultivation, the air-temperature approximation is generally acceptable.

**VPD targets by grow stage (indicative — facility-specific calibration required):**

| Stage | VPD Range |
|---|---|
| Propagation (cuttings) | 0.4 – 0.8 kPa |
| Early veg | 0.8 – 1.1 kPa |
| Late veg | 1.0 – 1.3 kPa |
| Early flower | 1.1 – 1.4 kPa |
| Late flower | 1.2 – 1.5 kPa |

The same VPD measurement carries different risk implications depending on grow stage. A VPD of 1.5 kPa in propagation is acute stress requiring immediate correction. The same 1.5 kPa in late flower is within the upper acceptable range. This stage-awareness must be hard-coded into the recommendation engine's interpretation logic.

### 6.2 Daily Light Integral Accumulation

Daily Light Integral (DLI) is the total photosynthetically active radiation delivered to the canopy over a 24-hour period, expressed in mol m⁻² d⁻¹.

**DLI calculation from instantaneous PPFD (µmol m⁻² s⁻¹):**

```
DLI = ∫₀^86400 PPFD(t) dt * 10⁻⁶  [mol m⁻² d⁻¹]
```

In practice, with 60-second sensor sampling:

```python
dli_today = sensor_data.query("date == today")["ppfd"].sum() * 60 * 1e-6
# units: µmol m⁻² s⁻¹ * 60 s * 1e-6 mol/µmol = mol m⁻²
```

DLI is a better predictor of photosynthesis and yield than instantaneous PPFD because it captures the integrated light dose. A batch that receives PPFD = 600 µmol m⁻² s⁻¹ for 18 hours achieves DLI = 38.9 mol m⁻² d⁻¹. The same batch with 600 µmol m⁻² s⁻¹ for 12 hours achieves only DLI = 25.9 mol m⁻² d⁻¹ — a 33% reduction in photosynthetic input.

**DLI targets by grow stage (indicative):**

| Stage | DLI Target |
|---|---|
| Propagation | 10 – 15 mol m⁻² d⁻¹ |
| Vegetative | 25 – 35 mol m⁻² d⁻¹ |
| Flower | 35 – 45 mol m⁻² d⁻¹ |

**Cumulative DLI deficit** (integral of daily shortfall from target over the batch) is a strong feature for batch outcome prediction: batches that consistently fall short of DLI targets tend to produce lower yield and potentially lower cannabinoid concentrations.

### 6.3 EC / pH Interaction with Rootzone Temperature

Electrical conductivity is temperature-dependent. Most EC meters apply a temperature correction to normalise readings to a standard temperature (typically 25°C):

```
EC_corrected = EC_measured / (1 + 0.02 * (T_rootzone - 25))
```

Failure to apply temperature correction leads to apparent EC variation that is actually driven by rootzone temperature fluctuation, confusing the model and masking true nutrient concentration changes.

pH affects nutrient availability through solubility: iron, manganese, zinc, copper, and boron are less available above pH 6.5; calcium and magnesium are less available below pH 5.5. The interaction between pH excursions and nutrient deficiency symptoms has a time lag of 24–72 hours, meaning that a pH excursion on day N manifests as visible deficiency on day N+2 to N+4. This lag must be captured through lag features in the model.

The AquaPro dosing unit (AQU1AD04A42) logs pH adjustment events with timestamp, adjustment direction (acid/base), and dose volume. These events are valuable features: a high frequency of pH adjustment events signals poor pH stability, which may indicate reservoir contamination, temperature-driven pH drift, or degradation of pH buffer capacity.

### 6.4 Substrate Moisture Dynamics (VWC)

Volumetric Water Content (VWC) in the substrate follows a characteristic sawtooth pattern: gradual drawdown as plants transpire, followed by rapid recovery after irrigation events. The shape of this curve encodes physiological information:

- **Drawdown rate:** Proxy for plant transpiration rate. High drawdown rate = high transpiration = high VPD stress or high light load.
- **Field capacity:** Maximum VWC after irrigation. Declining field capacity over successive irrigations may indicate substrate compaction or root zone development changes.
- **Irrigation timing:** The AquaPro triggers irrigations based on VWC threshold or time schedule. The system captures these events and can derive irrigation frequency as a feature.
- **Drainage fraction:** If drain-to-waste monitoring is available, the ratio of applied irrigation volume to drainage volume indicates substrate saturation status.

The within-day VWC trajectory is a candidate feature for real-time inference: a batch where VWC drops faster than expected for the current PPFD and VPD conditions may indicate developing root zone issues (root damage, pythium pressure) before visual symptoms appear.

### 6.5 Stage-Aware Feature Interpretation

The most important modelling principle specific to cultivation is that **the same sensor value has different meaning at different grow stages**. This must be handled explicitly in every modelling decision.

The recommended approach is to compute all rolling and summary features **within grow stage windows**, not across the entire batch duration. This means the feature `vpd_mean_rolling_7d` is actually three separate features: `vpd_mean_rolling_7d_veg`, `vpd_mean_rolling_7d_early_flower`, `vpd_mean_rolling_7d_late_flower`. Each carries a distinct importance weight in the model.

Additionally, stage transition timing is itself a feature. Premature transition from vegetative to flower stage (triggered too early) results in plants that are too small to support a full canopy at harvest. This is detectable as: `days_in_veg < threshold` combined with a specific `canopy_height_at_flip` metric (if available from manual records).
